Maybe you didn't land here by accident.

Maybe you've been thinking about taking a trip. Maybe you've been craving movement. Maybe something in you is tired of standing still.

We chose to build a life on the road — eight years of van life, all 48 continental states, eight months across Southeast Asia, and years of independent travel before that. Backpacking Europe. Living on an island in the Caribbean. Learning that movement changes you long before you understand how.

Not because we had certainty. But because we kept answering the pull.

## Why it took us eight years to hit publish

We have been living in a van for eight years.

But honestly, the van is just the latest chapter.

Between us, we've backpacked through Europe, lived in the Caribbean, traveled through South Africa, and spent eight months moving slowly through Southeast Asia — Thailand, Laos, Vietnam, Cambodia, and beyond. We've been figuring out how to build a life around movement for most of our adult lives. The van just made it official.

And for eight years of van life, we talked about writing it down.

We talked about it in the beginning, when everything was still terrifying and new and we were googling things like "how do you shower in a van" at 11pm in a Walmart parking lot. We talked about it in year three, when we finally felt like we knew what we were doing. We talked about it after Southeast Asia, when we came home to the van with more stories than we knew what to do with.

We always had a reason to wait. We weren't ready. We didn't have the right setup. We'd start after the next trip.

But that wasn't really it.

The truth is, starting a blog isn't like building a website. It's opening a door. And once you open it, strangers walk through — into your mornings, your mistakes, your actual life. That's a strange thing to invite. It took a long time to be okay with it.

Here's something we've noticed after years of being online while living this life: the version of it that gets shown most often isn't the real one. The van door slides open to a jaw-dropping view. The coffee is already made. Everything is golden and intentional and clean.

And sure — that morning exists. We've had it. It's real.

But we've had a lot more mornings that look like this: water slowly coming to a boil on the stove, moving the pile of stuff from one seat to another just to find what we're looking for, parked on a side street in a city we don't know yet, with no real idea what the day is going to look like.

Those mornings are real too. Maybe more real. And almost nobody shows you those.

We don't want to just add to the noise. We want to be the thing that makes you feel a little less alone in whatever you're figuring out.
