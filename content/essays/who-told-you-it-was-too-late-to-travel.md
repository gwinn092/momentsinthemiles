I've been asked some version of this question more times than I can count.

Not always directly. Sometimes it comes dressed as concern. Shouldn't you be saving more? Don't you want to settle down? Isn't it time to be practical? Sometimes it's just a look — the slight pause when you mention you're leaving again, the careful neutrality of someone who thinks you're making a mistake but has decided not to say so out loud.

The question underneath all of it is the same: at some point, doesn't this become irresponsible?

I used to feel the need to defend the choice. To explain the finances, justify the timeline, prove that we'd thought it through. To earn the right to live the way we were already living.

I don't do that anymore. Because I've come to believe the question is aimed at the wrong person.

## The question nobody's asking

Here's what I want to ask back:

Is it irresponsible not to?

Not recklessly. Not without thought. But deliberately, intentionally, with full awareness of what you're choosing and what you're trading — is it irresponsible to keep deferring the life you actually want?

Because there's a cost to waiting that nobody puts on the spreadsheet. It doesn't show up in your savings account or your retirement projections. It accumulates quietly, in the form of weeks that blur into months, in the slow narrowing of what feels possible, in the plans you keep moving to later until later stops being a real place.

We talk about financial irresponsibility constantly. We almost never talk about the other kind.

## The camper that never happened

I grew up two houses down from my grandparents in upstate New York. They were my constants — the people who took me on cross-country road trips, weekend camping adventures, long drives with no real destination. They showed me early that the world was bigger than our small town and that movement was something to be sought, not feared.

For as long as I can remember, they had a plan. Work hard. Save carefully. Retire well. Then — then — they'd buy the camper. Travel the country. Finally have the time to truly live.

Retirement was when life would begin.

My grandmother got sick before that chapter ever opened. She passed away. And after that, my grandfather was never quite the same. I watched decades of careful planning fail to guarantee more time. I watched the camper that never happened become the symbol of everything they'd saved for and never got to spend.

It was the hardest season of my life. And it reshaped me permanently.

There is no guarantee of later. That truth isn't morbid — it's clarifying.

It doesn't mean spend everything and plan nothing. It means the version of responsibility that requires you to postpone living indefinitely isn't actually responsible. It's just familiar.

## We made the van decision in our 30s

This is worth saying plainly, because it cuts against the story people tell about who gets to do this.

We didn't move into the van at 22, fresh out of college with no obligations and nothing to lose. We made that decision as adults — with full awareness of what it cost and what it gave back. Seven years of van life. All 48 continental states. A life built deliberately, not impulsively.

Then Southeast Asia. Eight months of slow travel through Thailand, Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, and the Philippines — not a gap year, not a crisis, but a conscious extension of a life we'd been building for years.

None of it happened because we were young enough not to know better. It happened because we were old enough to know exactly what we were choosing — and to choose it anyway.

There is no right age for this. There is only the age you are and the decision in front of you.

## Responsibility and fear are not the same thing

This is the distinction that matters most, and it almost never gets made.

There is real financial responsibility — building a safety net, managing debt thoughtfully, making decisions you can actually sustain. We believe in all of that. We've written about it. We've lived it.

And then there is financial fear dressed up as responsibility — the anxiety that no number in a savings account will ever feel like enough, that any money spent on living your life is money you should have kept, that security is something you approach asymptotically and never actually reach.

One of those is wisdom. The other is paralysis wearing responsible clothing.

The question isn't whether you can afford to travel. For most people genuinely asking this question, the answer — when they actually run the numbers — is yes, with planning. The real question is whether you've given yourself permission to want it.

That permission doesn't come from a financial milestone. It comes from a decision.

## What staying costs you

We talk endlessly about the cost of going. We almost never talk about the cost of staying.

Staying — out of hesitation, out of habit, out of the low hum of what everyone expects — has a price too. It's just paid in a different currency.

It's paid in the version of yourself that doesn't develop because the circumstances that would have shaped it never came. In the conversations you never had with strangers in places that weren't home. In the way your sense of what's possible slowly contracts when it isn't regularly expanded.

It's paid in the quiet, accumulating awareness that there were things you wanted and kept choosing not to pursue — until one day the wanting fades and you mistake the silence for contentment.

We're not saying travel fixes everything. It doesn't. We've never claimed that.

But we are saying that the cost of going is visible and the cost of staying is invisible — and invisible costs are the ones that catch you by surprise.

## The irresponsibility of the unlived life

My grandparents weren't irresponsible people. They were careful, loving, devoted. They did everything right by the standards they'd been given.

They just believed — as so many of us are taught to believe — that living comes after. After the work is done. After the savings are full. After the obligations are met. After.

What I carry from them isn't resentment. It's a lesson they gave me without meaning to, at a cost I would never have chosen for them.

The lesson is this: the responsible thing and the safe thing are not always the same thing. Sometimes the most responsible choice you can make is to stop treating your life as a dress rehearsal.

You are not being irresponsible by choosing a life with travel in it. You are not being reckless by choosing a life with movement in it. You are not behind because you haven't followed the script that was handed to you before you knew enough to question it.

What would be irresponsible is knowing what you want, having the means to pursue it, watching the window open — and choosing, year after year, to wait.

The camper never happened. The road is still here.

Go while you can.
