There is definitely one thing every person who visits Hanoi is going to have: a Bánh Mì.

You'll find one on every corner. Several, actually. Each claiming to be the best. The first. Award-winning. Original recipe. Family secret.

It's hard to make the right choice.

But here's what I've learned after years of traveling: if you want a great local spot, look for what's busy. Not just busy with tourists — busy with locals. Watch where people linger. Where they laugh. Where they come back.

The first time we found it in Hanoi, it was a busy evening. We had left our hotel with no plan — just hungry and curious. The streets were alive in that way Hanoi does so well. Motorbikes weaving through impossible gaps. Conversations layered over each other. The air thick with heat and something sizzling nearby.

What caught my eye first was a magnificent tall yellow tree stretching high above the street. Then I noticed the line. Groups of people shoulder to shoulder. Laughter spilling onto the sidewalk. And the tiny plastic stools — blue and red — scattered everywhere like they'd simply landed there and stayed.

That's when we saw it: Bami Bread — Bánh Mì.

That became our spot.

It was always busy. Always humming. Run by the sweetest girls — constantly moving, constantly smiling — efficient without ever feeling rushed. Bread cracking open. Hands moving fast and sure. Orders flying.

We kept coming back.

On our last visit, they were especially slammed. And because I've worked in the restaurant business for years, I recognized the rhythm immediately — the controlled chaos, the silent communication, the pride in getting it right.

Instead of hovering impatiently out front, we smiled. We waited. We met their pace with respect.

And something shifted.

They waved us behind the counter while we waited.

So there we sat on our tiny blue and red plastic stools, tucked into the kitchen behind the scenes, sipping cold Bia Hà Nội and watching the rhythm unfold from the inside.

The crack of fresh bread splitting open. The quick swipe of pâté. A fistful of herbs tossed in without measuring, without hesitation. The girls moving around each other like they'd done it a thousand times — because they had.

It was loud. Focused. Alive.

And what struck me wasn't just how good the sandwich tasted. It was how uncomplicated it felt.

No one was trying to optimize the experience. No one was cross-checking reviews. No one was wondering if there was something better two streets over.

They were just there. Hungry. Laughing. Eating.

And for a moment, so were we.

We weren't chasing the "best" bánh mì in Hanoi. We weren't curating a perfect travel story. We were participating.

Back home — whether that's New York, Colorado, or wherever the season takes us — I notice how quickly I slip back into managing life instead of living it.

Researching. Comparing. Structuring. Optimizing. Even lunch can feel like something that needs to be done correctly.

But in that kitchen in Hanoi, there was no correct choice. There was just presence.

I don't think travel makes life better. I think it makes certain things more visible.

Like how often I stand just outside the counter, waiting to be served, instead of stepping fully into the moment in front of me.

Sometimes I think about those blue and red stools — how low they sit, how close to the street, how nothing about them is polished or impressive.

And I realize that I don't want a bigger life. I want a closer one.

Closer to the street. Closer to the people in front of me. Closer to the rhythm instead of outside of it.

I'm not perfect at it. But I see it now.

And that feels like something worth keeping.
