The night before we left, we had a bonfire.

My Aunt Marcee's backyard in Lone Tree. Family crowded around the fire, passing food, talking over each other the way our family does. Someone took a group photo — the kind where everyone smiles a little too wide because nobody knows quite what else to do. And at some point in the night I found myself sitting next to my little cousin Ryder.

He was nine. Quiet the way smart kids are quiet — taking everything in, missing nothing. We'd done a lot together, Ryder and me. His first hike. His first camping trip. The first fish he ever caught, the first fire he ever built, the first s'more that collapsed in his hands before he could get it to his mouth. I was always trying to show him — without making a big thing of it — that the world was bigger than what he could see from where he was standing.

That night I didn't know how to explain what we were about to do or why. So I didn't try.

We just sat there by the fire.

We left before dawn.

I remember the neighborhood was completely still — that specific Colorado quiet where the air is thin and cool and the sky starts turning before the sun has any right to be up yet. The van was packed. Everything we needed, and nothing we didn't, loaded into seventy square feet of space that was about to become our entire world.

I cried pulling out of that driveway.

I'm not sure I expected to. I was excited — genuinely, fully excited. Florida was waiting. The road was waiting. The life we had been building toward was finally, actually happening. But I've learned something about myself since then, something that trip began to teach me: I cry when I leave things that fill me. Not from sadness exactly — from fullness. From the particular ache of walking away from something that touched you, that connected you, that made you feel like the best version of yourself.

It's the closest thing I know to what Anthony Bourdain meant when he said the journey changes you. That it should change you.

The leaving is part of it. It has to be.

We didn't know how it was going to turn out.

I want to be honest about that, because I think we do a disservice to people who are still deciding when we tell our stories only in hindsight — when everything is already resolved, already meaningful, already worth it. Standing in that driveway in the dark, I had no idea what the next chapter looked like. I just knew that not going felt worse than the uncertainty of going.

That's not courage. That's just honesty.

There's a myth that the people who make bold choices feel certain when they make them. That one day clarity arrives, the numbers line up, the doubts go quiet, and everyone nods in approval.

That has never once been our experience.

We were excited and scared and sad to say goodbye, all at the same time, standing in a driveway in Lone Tree, Colorado at four in the morning. We didn't have a guarantee. We had a van, a direction, and each other.

It turned out to be enough.

Seven years. All 48 continental states. Tens of thousands of miles of road that looked like nothing on a map and everything in person. Desert light in Utah at golden hour, the kind that makes you pull over whether you planned to or not. The particular silence of a forest in the Pacific Northwest, so deep it has its own sound. Storm systems moving across the plains so big you can watch them from miles away, tracking them like something alive.

The miles gave us all of that.

But more than the places — and this is the part that's harder to explain — they gave us ourselves. A clearer version. A more honest one. Road life strips away the performance of ordinary life. There's no keeping up with anyone in seventy square feet. There's no autopilot. You find out quickly what you actually need, what you can genuinely let go of, and what kind of person you are when comfort stops being guaranteed.

We didn't always like what we found. But we needed to see it.

That's what the miles do, if you let them.

I still think about Ryder at that bonfire. He's sixteen now — not so little anymore. Still quiet. Still watching. Still the kind of person who doesn't miss much.

I try to show this to my whole family. But it matters most with the little ones, because they're still early enough in their lives that it can actually change the shape of things. What society tells you that you're supposed to do — the script you're handed before you're old enough to question it — that is not the only option. There are other ways to build something. Other definitions of a life well lived.

I hope those years on the road showed Ryder something of that. Not that he has to live the way we do — he doesn't, and that's not the point. But that choices exist outside the ones you were handed. That a life can be built on purpose, not just assembled by default. That the world is genuinely bigger than the view from where you're standing right now.

I needed someone to show me that once too.

You don't have to move into a van. You don't have to cross an ocean. You don't have to blow up the life you have in order to find the one you want.

But staying exactly where you are — out of hesitation rather than intention — shapes you too. That's the part we don't talk about enough.

Stillness is a choice. So is movement. And both of them change you, whether you're paying attention or not.

The hardest part of that morning wasn't the logistics. It wasn't the packed van or the pre-dawn alarm or even the goodbye. It was the moment just before — sitting with the fact that we had decided, and that deciding meant it was real, and that real meant we were responsible for whatever came next.

That moment is the one nobody can do for you.

But here's what I know from the other side of it: clarity didn't come before the miles. It came because of them. We weren't ready when we left. We became ready on the road.

If something in you is pulling — if some part of you is tired of waiting for permission — listen to it.

You don't have to be certain. You just have to be willing.

Pull out of the driveway. Let yourself cry if you need to.

And go see what the miles make of you.

The road doesn't promise ease. But it does offer perspective — if you let it.
