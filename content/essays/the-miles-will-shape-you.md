How do you even decide where to go?

There are endless options. Countries. States. Islands. Cities. Mountains or oceans. Familiar or foreign.

You don't have to cross oceans. You don't have to move into a van. You don't have to leave everything behind.

But staying exactly where you are — out of hesitation rather than intention — shapes you too. That's the part we don't talk about enough.

We tend to think movement is the bold choice and staying is the safe one. But sometimes staying is simply easier. Sometimes it's quieter. Sometimes it's what everyone expects. And sometimes it slowly narrows you without you even noticing.

For years, we've made choices that didn't look conventional from the outside. We independently traveled through Europe in our early adulthood. One of us spent five years living on the island of St. John. We later chose to live in seventy square feet on wheels. And then, in another season, we left again — this time for Southeast Asia.

None of those decisions arrived with perfect clarity. We were never fully ready. We were simply willing to begin.

There's a myth that courage feels like certainty. That one day you wake up knowing it's time — that the numbers line up, the doubts disappear, and everyone nods in approval.

That's not how it's ever worked for us.

Clarity didn't come before the miles. It came because of them.

Traveling alone in your twenties feels bold. It's expected, even celebrated. You're "figuring things out." You're "finding yourself."

Choosing a life built around movement later — choosing it intentionally, repeatedly — requires something quieter.

Not recklessness. Not rebellion. Conviction.

Conviction that growth doesn't have an expiration date. Conviction that curiosity is still worth following. Conviction that stability and movement are not opposites — they just look different for different people.

The miles don't fix you. They reveal you.

They expose your impatience when a train is delayed. They test your adaptability when plans unravel. They magnify tension in a partnership — and also deepen it. They clarify what you actually need versus what you thought you needed.

If you let them.

That last part matters. You can travel the world and remain unchanged. You can also cross a single state line and come back seeing your life differently.

The geography isn't the point. The willingness is.

Before one of our bigger departures, there was a quiet moment of doubt. Not dramatic. Not panicked. Just a steady internal question: are we supposed to have settled by now?

It's a subtle pressure — the one that suggests growth has a timeline. That adventure has an age bracket. That choosing something unconventional eventually becomes irresponsible.

But here's what we've learned: there is no age limit on curiosity. Only on denying it.

The miles will not magically transform your life. They won't solve dissatisfaction or erase uncertainty. They won't hand you purpose in a neatly wrapped package.

What they will do is create space. Space to see your routines clearly. Space to notice what drains you. Space to recognize what energizes you. Space to decide more intentionally.

And sometimes that space is all you needed.

This doesn't require a one-way ticket or a radical overhaul. Start smaller. Take the long weekend. Work remotely from a new city for a month. Cross a state line before crossing an ocean. Run the numbers before romanticizing the lifestyle. Build a safety net. Ask better questions. Move incrementally.

Courage scales.

The hardest part is rarely the logistics. It's the decision. It's admitting you want something different. It's choosing movement when stillness would be easier to explain.

You don't have to burn your life down. You don't have to outrun who you are.

You just have to move far enough outside your routine to see yourself clearly.

Because stillness shapes you. But so do the miles.

And if you let them — they won't just change where you are. They'll change how you live.
