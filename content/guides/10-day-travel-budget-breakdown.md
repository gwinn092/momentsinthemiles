Here's something most travel blogs won't tell you upfront: Southeast Asia is cheaper than your monthly car payment.

We know because we lived it. Eight months across Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Laos — and by the time we did the honest math, the trip cost less than staying home would have. That's not a sales pitch. It's just geography, and a willingness to look at the actual numbers before assuming you can't afford it.

So here they are. Real numbers. Current figures. A genuine 10-day breakdown for two people traveling Southeast Asia in 2025 — the way we travel. Not luxury, not rock-bottom suffer-mode. Comfortable, immersive, real.

## FIRST, THE CONTEXT

We're not backpackers in the 22-years-old-sleeping-in-dorms sense. We want private rooms. We want to eat well. We want the occasional cold beer without counting every baht. We travel slowly — fewer places, more time — which almost always costs less than rushing between destinations.

This breakdown reflects that style. If you're happy with dorm beds and street food only, subtract 20–30%. If you want boutique hotels and nightly restaurant dinners, add 40–50%. The framework stays the same either way.

For this breakdown, we're anchoring in Thailand and Vietnam — two countries we know well, with some Laos context for comparison. Prices are in USD and reflect current 2025–2026 conditions.

## THE DAILY BREAKDOWN: TWO PEOPLE, 10 DAYS

**Accommodation — $25–$45/night total**

Private rooms in family-run guesthouses run $15–$30 a night across most of Thailand and Vietnam. In Laos, you can find clean, comfortable rooms for even less. In Chiang Mai, we regularly stayed in lovely spots for under $20 a night. In Hanoi and Hoi An, $25–$35 gets you something genuinely charming — sometimes with breakfast included.

Vietnam tends to run 20–30% cheaper than Thailand across most categories. Private rooms outside capital cities generally fall between $12–$25 a night.

Budget: $30/night average for two → $300 for 10 nights

**Food — $25–$40/day for two**

This is where Southeast Asia genuinely defies logic if you're used to Western prices.

Street meals — pad thai, pho, bánh mì — run $1–$2 each. Sit-down restaurant mains land at $4–$8. We eat street food for most breakfasts and lunches, then allow ourselves a proper sit-down dinner most nights. Cold local beers — Singha in Thailand, Bia Hà Nội in Vietnam, BeerLao in Laos — run $1–$2 a bottle.

A real day of eating for two people — coffee, two street breakfasts, a proper lunch, dinner at a local restaurant, two beers each — comes in around $20–$30 without trying hard.

Budget: $30/day for two → $300 for 10 days

**Local transport — $5–$15/day for two**

Grab makes city transport inexpensive and scam-proof. Scooter rentals run $5–$8 per day if you're comfortable riding — and they're worth it. Between cities, sleeper buses and night trains run $10–$30 for long hauls — and an overnight bus means you're saving a night of accommodation at the same time.

Budget: $10/day average for two → $100 for 10 days

**Activities — $5–$20/day for two**

Many of the best experiences in Southeast Asia are free or nearly free. Temples. Markets. Wandering. The things that stick with you rarely cost much. We budget deliberately for a few paid experiences — a cooking class, a boat tour, a guided trek — and spread them across the trip. Jungle trekking with a guide generally runs $30–$50 per day total. Temple entry, where it's not free, is typically $1–$5.

Budget: $10/day average for two → $100 for 10 days

**Buffer — visas, SIM cards, the unexpected**

Visa fees, entrance fees, SIM cards, and small surprises add roughly $3–$6 per person per day when averaged across a trip. Vietnam's e-visa runs around $25 per person. A local SIM costs $5–$10 for the trip. Factor in a small emergency cushion and you're covered.

Budget: $100 flat for the trip for two

That's $45 per person per day, all in.

Add international flights — budget $700–$1,100 per person return from the US depending on flexibility and timing — and a 10-day trip for two comes in somewhere between $2,500 and $3,200 total, flights included.

On flights: the difference between the low and high end of that range usually comes down to two things — timing and tools. We book 6–8 weeks out for the best combination of availability and price, and we use Skyscanner and Going to track fares and catch deals before they disappear. We put everything on our Capital One Venture X card and earn travel points on every dollar spent. Over a trip like this, the points add up to meaningful money toward the next one.

On travel insurance: it's not in the table above because the cost varies, but don't skip it. A basic policy for two people covering a 10-day international trip typically runs $50–$100. Given what a single medical evacuation or cancelled flight can cost, it's the most rational line item in the entire budget.

## WHAT MOVES THE NUMBER UP OR DOWN

Slower travel costs less. The biggest budget leak in Southeast Asia isn't food or accommodation — it's transportation between places. Every time you move cities, you spend. Stay longer in fewer spots and the daily average drops noticeably. This is the single most effective thing you can do for your budget, and it makes the trip better at the same time.

Islands cost more than the mainland. Popular Thai islands like Phuket, Koh Phi Phi, and Koh Samui can run 25–50% more than mainland prices. Factor this in if beach time is part of the plan.

Peak season adds cost. Costs can run up to 30% higher during peak season (December through February in Thailand). Shoulder season — March through May, September through October — offers the best combination of lower prices and manageable crowds.

Couples have a structural advantage. A double room typically costs only 10–20% more than a single, effectively cutting per-person accommodation costs nearly in half compared to solo travel.

## THE HONEST COMPARISON

Here's what we kept coming back to during those eight months: the math wasn't just favorable. It was almost embarrassing.

The cost of two people living and traveling through Southeast Asia for a month was comparable to — sometimes less than — what two people spend on rent, groceries, and routine expenses in many American cities. Not a vacation splurge. A lifestyle that happened to cost less than the one we'd left behind.

We're not saying it's for everyone. We're saying: run the actual numbers before you decide it's out of reach.

The world is cheaper than most people assume. They just never check.
