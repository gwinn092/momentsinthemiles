We didn't plan to spend eight months in Southeast Asia. We planned to spend three, maybe four. But that's the thing about this part of the world — it keeps offering you one more reason to stay, one more bus to catch, one more village you've never heard of that turns out to be the one you'll talk about for years.

We arrived in Bangkok in the fall with a rough route and no fixed end date. We left eight months later from a tiny airport in Bali, heading to Switzerland to reunite with two friends we'd met on a motorbike loop in northern Vietnam — people we hadn't known existed when we landed. That felt like the right ending. Or maybe not an ending at all.

This is the full story: every country, every highlight, and the moments in between that don't make the Instagram grid but make the trip. If you're planning your own time in Southeast Asia, we hope this gives you something real to hold onto.

---

## Thailand

### Bangkok — 8 days

Bangkok was supposed to be an orientation. A place to land softly, shake off the jet lag, figure out how to eat noodles standing up at 7am. And it was all of those things. But it was also immediately, overwhelmingly itself — dense and golden and chaotic in a way that makes you feel very small and very alive at the same time.

Eight days felt like both too long and not nearly enough. We wandered temples in the early morning before the heat settled in, ate our way through street food markets, and started to understand the rhythm that would carry us through the next eight months: wake up early, move slowly, say yes to things you don't entirely understand yet.

### Chiang Mai — 26 days

We didn't intend to stay in Chiang Mai for nearly a month. We booked a week and then kept extending. It's that kind of place.

{{< photo src="/images/guides/thailand-chiang-mai.jpg" alt="A golden temple and motorbike traffic in Chiang Mai" caption="Chiang Mai" >}}

We moved through four different neighborhoods during our stay — Phra Singh, Hai Ya, Chang Khlan, and in and around the old city — and each one had its own texture. The old town with its moat and crumbling temple walls felt almost like a village. Chang Khlan buzzed with the Night Bazaar. Hai Ya was quieter, residential, the kind of place where you find a coffee shop that becomes yours.

The reason we were there for 26 days was partly the city itself and partly timing. We were there for the Yi Peng lantern festival — Loi Krathong — and if you've ever seen a photograph of thousands of paper lanterns lifting into a dark sky above a city and wondered if it could possibly be as beautiful in person, the answer is that it is more beautiful. It is one of the most extraordinary things we have ever witnessed. We stood in a crowd of strangers and launched a lantern together and didn't say anything for a long time.

We also spent a day in the Mae Wang District to see elephants — ethically, at a sanctuary where they roam freely and you walk among them rather than ride them. It's one of those experiences that rearranges something in you quietly.

### Pai — 1 week

A van carried us up through 762 curves of mountain road to Pai, a small town in the hills that sits somewhere between backpacker haven and genuine escape. We rented motorbikes and explored for a week.

We want to say this clearly: if you have been curious about riding a motorbike but haven't done it yet, Pai is the place to learn. The roads are manageable, the traffic is forgiving, and the scenery is the kind that rewards you for moving slowly. By the end of the week we felt like we'd always known how to do it. That confidence would carry us a long way — including onto the Ha Giang Loop months later in Vietnam.

### Chiang Rai & Chiang Khong

From Pai we made our way to Chiang Rai, stopping to see the White Temple — Wat Rong Khun — and the Blue Temple — Wat Rong Suea Ten. Neither photograph quite prepares you. The White Temple is all reflective glass and intricate white plasterwork; the Blue Temple glows electric in the late afternoon. We spent more time than we planned and didn't regret it.

Chiang Khong was the edge of Thailand — a small border town on the Mekong where you catch the boat into Laos. We arrived expecting to spend one night. But the town had a quiet quality we liked, and by then we were learning to trust that instinct.

## Laos

### The slow boat — Pakbeng & beyond

The slow boat from the Thai border into Laos is a two-day journey down the Mekong on a long wooden vessel with rows of seats and views that scroll past like a painting you can't quite believe is real. It is one of the most recommended experiences in Southeast Asia, and the recommendations are earned.

{{< photo src="/images/guides/laos-sunset.jpg" alt="Sunset over the Mekong with longboats" caption="Sunset on the Mekong" >}}

We were supposed to stop one night in Pakbeng, the small river town at the halfway point, and continue to Luang Prabang the next morning.

We stayed three.

The reason was a man named Vieng. We met him the first evening, introduced by the particular kind of ease that comes from being strangers in a place where everyone is a stranger. He was from the town. He knew everyone. He took us to dinner, to a local bar, to a part of Pakbeng that most people on the slow boat never see because they're back at their guesthouse recovering for the next morning's departure.

We canceled our onward tickets. We stayed. We saw the town through someone else's eyes, which is the only way to really see a place. What should have been a transit stop became three days that we still talk about more than almost anywhere else we went.

Vieng, if you ever read this: thank you.

### Luang Prabang — 12 days

Luang Prabang is the kind of beautiful that feels almost unfair. It sits on a peninsula between two rivers, its streets lined with French colonial architecture and saffron-robed monks and temples that glow in the evening light. Every morning the monks walk in procession through the town at dawn for the alms-giving ceremony — tak bat — and if you witness it with the quiet it deserves, it will stay with you.

We spent 12 days there, which felt about right. We rented bicycles and rode to waterfalls. We climbed Phu Si hill at sunset. We ate laap and sticky rice and drank Beer Lao on slow evenings by the water. We got good at doing very little very well.

Then we got on a 27-hour bus to Vietnam.

## Vietnam

### Hanoi — first stay, 5 days

Hanoi arrives loudly. After the gentleness of Laos, the capital of Vietnam is a city that insists on itself — motorbikes moving in every direction at once, sidewalks that function as parking lots and restaurants simultaneously, a street food culture so good and so cheap it becomes difficult to eat anywhere indoors.

We spent five days getting our bearings, eating bun cha and pho and egg coffee, wandering Hoan Kiem Lake in the early morning, and preparing for what came next.

### The Ha Giang Loop — the highlight of the trip

We took a bus from Hanoi to Vi Xuyen — Thuan Hoa, near the Chinese border in far northern Vietnam — and rented motorbikes for four days on the Ha Giang Loop.

We want to be careful here not to over-describe it, because some things lose something in description. But we'll try.

The Ha Giang Loop is a circuit through some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in Southeast Asia — limestone karsts rising straight from the valley floors, rice terraces cut into steep hillsides, passes that put you above the clouds and below them alternately as you ride. The roads are narrow and winding and occasionally terrifying. The villages are small and the people are Hmong and Tay and Lo Lo. You ride through places that feel genuinely remote in a world that has very few genuinely remote places left.

We rode for four days. We got lost twice and didn't mind. We pushed our bikes up a hill we weren't ready for. We stopped at a viewpoint and sat there longer than we meant to because neither of us wanted to be the one to say we should keep moving.

And we met Daniel and Serena.

They were doing the loop the same days we were, in the other direction. We crossed paths at a guesthouse on the second evening, had dinner together, stayed up later than we should have. By the end of the trip we knew we'd made the kind of friends you don't plan for. We didn't know yet that we'd end the entire eight months on their couch in Switzerland.

### Hanoi — second stay & Jesse gets sick

We came back to Hanoi and spent a week there. The weather was bad — gray and cold in a way that northern Vietnam gets in winter that surprises most visitors who've imagined only tropical heat. We hunkered down.

And then Jesse got sick.

We won't bury the lead: it was serious enough that we ended up at a hospital in Hanoi, which was our first experience navigating medical care abroad. It was disorienting and expensive and, ultimately, fine — Jesse recovered, we had travel insurance that covered it, and the hospital was more capable than our initial panic gave it credit for. But it was a reminder that travel is not always the highlight reel. Sometimes it is a fluorescent-lit waiting room in a city where you don't speak the language, trying to communicate symptoms through a translation app while the person you love is in pain.

We got through it. We kept going. And we think it's worth writing about honestly because if you travel long enough, something will go wrong, and knowing that others have navigated it helps.

### Ninh Binh — 5 days (and Jesse's birthday)

We went to the Ninh Binh region next — a landscape sometimes called "Ha Long Bay on land," with limestone karsts rising from rice paddies and river valleys. We took a rowboat through the caves at Trang An and watched herons land in the fields outside our guesthouse window.

Jesse's birthday fell here. We had a good dinner and rented bicycles and rode through the paddies in the late afternoon. It was quiet and lovely and exactly the right place to mark the day.

### Hoi An — TET

We timed it right and arrived in Hoi An for TET, the Vietnamese Lunar New Year — the most important celebration in the Vietnamese calendar. Hoi An during TET is something specific: the old town draped in red and gold lanterns, the streets transformed, families reuniting, the whole city lit differently.

We spent a week there, eating cao lau and white rose dumplings, getting clothes made at the tailor shops the town is famous for, and sitting along the Thu Bon River in the evenings watching lanterns float downstream.

### Nha Trang, Da Lat & Ho Chi Minh City

We moved south through the country. Nha Trang for four days — beach city, beautiful bay, the kind of place where you rent a scooter and drive the coastal road and feel briefly like you are in a film. Da Lat for five days — a cooler hill town with French colonial architecture, strawberry farms, and a persistent drizzle that we made our peace with.

Then Ho Chi Minh City for a week. The energy was different from Hanoi — faster, more modern, less ancient-feeling but just as overwhelming. We ate and walked and visited the War Remnants Museum, which is not an easy afternoon but is a necessary one.

### The Mekong Delta — reunion

Daniel and Serena, our friends from the Ha Giang Loop, were passing through Vietnam. We arranged to meet and spent three days together in the Mekong Delta — a maze of river channels and floating markets and villages where life still runs along the water. It was good to see them. It felt like something clicking back into place.

We said goodbye assuming it was for a while. We were wrong about that in the best way.

### Phu Quoc — 3 days

A ferry to Phu Quoc, an island in the Gulf of Thailand near the Cambodian border. We needed a few days that were purely slow — beach, hammock, a book, not much else. Phu Quoc gave us that.

## Cambodia

### Sihanoukville, Koh Rong & back

We took a van from Phu Quoc into Cambodia, arriving in Sihanoukville for one night before catching a ferry the next day to Koh Rong.

Koh Rong is a large island with multiple beaches and a different vibe depending on where you land. We landed in the wrong part for us — it skewed younger, louder, more party-oriented, which is a perfectly fine thing for a beach island to be, just not what we were after. We stayed one night and came back.

And here's the thing: we loved our hotel in Sihanoukville. We extended two more nights purely because of it. Sometimes that's the whole story — you find a place that gets the details right and you don't need more reason than that.

### Back through Bangkok

A bus back into Bangkok for a week. We had suits made — something we'd been planning since Chiang Mai and finally had the timeline to do properly. Bangkok's tailors are excellent and the process, if you have the time, is worth it entirely. We walked out with clothes that fit in a way off-the-rack never quite manages.

## Southern Thailand

### Phuket & Krabi

A flight into Kathu, Phuket — five days on an island we'd heard polarizing things about. Phuket is large enough that it contains multitudes: yes, there is the strip of Patong and the roar of tourist infrastructure, but there are also quieter beaches and better food than its reputation suggests if you know where to look.

A ferry from Phuket to Ao Nang, Krabi — four days. Krabi is spectacular in a way that photographs still somehow undersell. The limestone cliffs dropping straight into green water, the longtail boats at sunrise, the beach at Railay that you can only reach by sea. We kayaked, we hiked, we ate too much seafood. We were sad to leave.

---

## Malaysia & Singapore

### Kuala Lumpur, Penang & Singapore

A flight into Kuala Lumpur for four days. KL is a genuinely great city — cosmopolitan, food-obsessed, the kind of place where you can eat your way through a dozen different culinary traditions without trying very hard.

A ferry to Penang — George Town — for three days. George Town might be our favorite city in Malaysia. UNESCO-listed for its historic architecture, covered in street art that somehow manages not to feel forced, home to one of the best food cultures in a country already renowned for its food. We ate char kway teow and asam laksa and cendol and wished we had more time.

Two more days back in KL before flying to Singapore for two days — which is not enough time for Singapore, frankly, but enough to eat at hawker centres, walk the colonial district, and feel the particular order and ambition of a city-state that decided to take everything seriously.

## The Philippines

### Manila, Bohol, Cebu & Palawan

The Philippines felt like a reset. After weeks of mainland and peninsula travel, we were suddenly on islands again — a different kind of light, a different pace, a specific warmth in the people that we noticed immediately.

Four days in Manila. Three days in Bohol, where the Chocolate Hills rise in hundreds of identical mounds from the flat land and tarsiers cling to branches in the dark with their enormous eyes. Four days in Cebu — we did the canyoneering in Kawasan Falls, which involves jumping off cliffs into turquoise water and swimming through canyons and is one of the most purely fun things we did in eight months.

Then Palawan. Puerto Princesa for a few days, the Underground River, then the long road north to Port Barton — four days in a village so small it barely registers on most maps, with a beach that justifies the journey entirely.

### El Nido & the four-day boat

El Nido is the kind of place that travel writing reaches for superlatives and runs out. The lagoons, the limestone karsts, the water that is genuinely that color. We spent three days there and then boarded a boat.

The four-day journey north through the Palawan archipelago is slow and occasionally uncomfortable and deeply, deeply worth it. You sleep on the boat. You wake up to different islands. You eat simple meals on deck and swim at stops and watch the stars in a way you can't on land.

By the time we reached Manila we felt like different people than the ones who boarded, which is a thing a good journey does to you.

## Indonesia

### Bali & Nusa Lembongan

We flew from Manila to Denpasar, Bali — arriving in the late afternoon and catching the ferry to Nusa Lembongan the next morning. Lembongan is a small island southeast of Bali, car-free and quieter, with mangroves and surf breaks and a pace that Bali itself has largely outgrown.

We stayed six days. We rented scooters and circled the island in an afternoon. We watched surfers from a cliff. We ate Indonesian food at warungs where the bill came to almost nothing and the food was extraordinary.

### Lombok & Senggigi

A boat to Senggigi on the island of Lombok — 11 days, our longest stop in Indonesia. Lombok sits in Bali's shadow in the tourist imagination but has its own complete identity: the rice paddies of the central highlands, the volcanic peak of Rinjani, the Sasak culture distinct from Bali's Hindu traditions. Senggigi is relaxed in a way that suited us after months of moving.

We settled. We cooked some meals. We made tentative friends with a cat that lived near our guesthouse. We needed that.

### Komodo & the islands

Two nights in Flores before the Komodo boat trip, which runs three days through the islands: Komodo Island and the dragons that move through the scrub in a way that makes you very aware that they are large predators; Pink Beach, which is genuinely pink from the coral; Padar Island at sunrise from a hilltop with the three bays below.

Then Gili Air — four days on the smallest of the three Gili Islands, no motorized vehicles, the kind of quiet where you can hear the ocean from anywhere on the island.

### Back to Bali — and the end

Two nights in Bali. We'd been moving for eight months. We sat on the beach and tried to account for all of it, which is impossible to do and also a good way to spend an afternoon.

Then we flew to Switzerland.

## Switzerland — the full circle

Daniel and Serena had been tracking us loosely through the months since the Ha Giang Loop. When we mentioned we were finishing, they said: come stay with us.

We spent 23 days in Switzerland. We hiked in the Alps and ate cheese and drank wine and slept in a real bed that didn't move and cooked dinner in an actual kitchen. We talked for hours about what it had been — all of it, the slow boat and the hospital in Hanoi and the four-day boat through the Philippines and the things we hadn't expected to feel.

It was the right way to close something. With people who understood the kind of trip it had been, in a place completely unlike anywhere we'd been, letting the eight months settle into us before we had to figure out what came next.

## What eight months in Southeast Asia taught us

We've had a lot of time to think about what the trip actually gave us, beyond the places themselves. Here's what feels true:

**The itinerary will be wrong, and that's the whole point.** We rerouted constantly — canceled tickets, extended stays, added countries. The best things that happened were not planned. Vieng in Pakbeng. Daniel and Serena on a mountainside in Vietnam. The hotel in Sihanoukville we'd never have found if we hadn't turned around. Build in space for the thing you can't schedule.

**Slow down more than you think you need to.** Twelve days in Luang Prabang. Eleven in Senggigi. A week in Pai. The places that became ours were the ones we stayed in long enough to stop being tourists in them. The temptation to cover more ground is always there and almost always wrong.

**Something will go wrong. It's not a reason not to go.** Jesse's hospital stay in Hanoi was frightening while it was happening and now is just a story — one we're glad to have gotten through and even glad to have, in the odd way that difficult experiences become part of you. Get good travel insurance. Have a plan. Know that you will figure it out.

**The people are the trip.** The landscapes in northern Vietnam are extraordinary. The temples in Luang Prabang are extraordinary. But what we actually remember, the things that surface when someone asks about the trip, are almost always people. Vieng. Daniel and Serena. The family who invited us for TET dinner in Hoi An. The woman running the warung in Lembongan who remembered our order. Pay attention to people.

**Eight months is both a long time and not enough.** We came home with a list of places we want to go back to longer than the list we started with. That feels right.
