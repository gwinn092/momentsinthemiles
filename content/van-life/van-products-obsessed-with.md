We've been living in a van for eight years. We know what works, what we'd redo, and what we wish had existed when we first started building. Lately, a handful of products have crossed our radar that stopped us mid-scroll. One of us holds up the phone to show the other and says: okay, but what if we rebuilt.

This isn't a roundup of the usual suspects. These are the finds that are actually changing what a van interior can look and feel like: more considered, more beautiful, more livable. Some we'd use on day one. Some are going straight into the "next build" folder. All of them are worth knowing about.

---

## ClickDeco tiles — the backsplash we didn't know we needed

If you've ever stared at a bare plywood wall behind your van kitchen and thought there has to be a better way: there is. And it requires zero tools, zero adhesive, and zero permanent commitment.

ClickDeco tiles (clickdeco.ca) are exactly what they sound like: interlocking backsplash tiles that click together like LEGO. 28 colors, two sizes, fully removable, and they look good. Not "van good." Just good.

We'd put these behind the kitchen counter, maybe as an accent strip along the bottom of a cabinet run. The kind of detail that makes someone peek inside and ask if that's real tile. For a rental build, a first-time build, or anyone who wants to upgrade their space without touching a single thing underneath, this is a no-brainer.

---

## Portola Paints Roman Clay — walls that feel like a home

The thing about most van walls is that they feel like van walls. Paneling, spray paint, peel-and-stick: even when it's done well, it reads as a workaround. Roman Clay is what happens when you stop working around the problem.

Portola Paints (portolapaints.com) makes a zero-VOC Roman Clay finish with 65+ colors that goes on with a putty knife and dries to a warm, matte, stone-like texture. The result looks like something out of an Architectural Digest feature. In a van.

It's DIY-friendly, and there's a topcoat option that adds a slight sheen and makes the surface wipeable, which matters in any kitchen-adjacent space. If we were starting from scratch, this is the wall treatment we'd reach for. The depth and warmth it adds is hard to replicate any other way.

If Roman Clay feels too polished for your vibe, Portola also makes a limewash version: softer, more weathered, more bohemian. Same brand, different mood. Both worth a look.

---

## Havelock Wool — insulation that actually belongs in a van

Insulation is one of those things nobody talks about until they're sweating through a July in the desert or waking up to condensation on every surface in November. We've done both. We have opinions.

Havelock Wool (havelockwool.com) is natural sheep wool insulation built specifically for van builds, and it shows. Chemical-free, moisture-managing (the real enemy in a closed metal box), and it cuts road noise in a way synthetic options don't. They have a dedicated van section on their site and ship nationally.

Not the flashiest product on this list. Earns its place every single day.

---

## VanLab & Lost Hiway — cabinets that were actually designed for a van

For a long time, building van cabinets meant either going full custom or adapting IKEA furniture that wasn't designed for this life. A couple of newer brands are changing that.

VanLab makes a modular cabinet system built specifically for vans: proportions that fit, mounting that accounts for the curves, configurations that make sense for how people actually live in these spaces. Lost Hiway Cabinetry is the premium end of the same idea: high-end, van-specific, built for when budget isn't the limiting factor.

We went full custom on our current build and don't regret it. But if we were weighing options today, both of these would be on the shortlist.

## Hydronic heating — hot water, hot air, and radiant floor heat. All at once.

This one is firmly in the next-build folder, but it's too good not to include.

A hydronic heating system does three things a standard diesel heater doesn't: it heats your air, heats your water, and runs radiant heat through your floor. The radiant floor heat alone (waking up to a warm floor in January) is enough to make a case. It's a bigger investment, but the efficiency and the comfort are on another level.

Eight years in, and the products available for van builds are better than when we started. The design is more intentional, more van-specific, with fewer things you have to MacGyver into a space they weren't made for.
