This is the question we get asked more than any other. More than how we find parking, more than what we do for showers, more than whether we're safe. People want to know: can I actually afford this? And we get it. We asked the same thing before we left.

Eight years in, we can finally give you an honest answer — not a polished highlight reel, but the real breakdown of what van life costs us right now, in 2025, in the middle of some of the highest inflation we've seen in years.

The short answer: it's highly variable. The longer answer is what this post is for.

## First, the honest caveat: it depends on how much you drive

Everything in van life costs hinge on one thing more than anything else — how many miles you're putting on. Fuel is your biggest swinging variable. A month where we're staying put, exploring one region, running errands efficiently looks completely different from a month where we're covering ground between states.

We've gotten strategic about this. We plan our outings to accomplish multiple things at once. We track gas prices and fill up at the cheaper stations. We don't drive just to drive. That discipline didn't come overnight — it came from years of paying attention.

## Let's talk about fuel

Current Colorado average: around $4/gallon. We budget $320–$400/month on fuel, but that assumes we're being intentional.

What does intentional look like? It means we don't make separate trips for separate things. Grocery run, errand, and a coffee stop happen in the same outing. We use GasBuddy to find the cheapest stations nearby. We don't idle. These feel like small things but they add up across a month, across a year, across eight years.

If you're in a season of heavy travel — crossing states, chasing weather, relocating — your fuel costs will be significantly higher. Plan for it.

## Overnight costs: mostly free, occasionally not

We're BLM land and street parking people. The majority of our nights cost us nothing. Public lands, dispersed camping, quiet residential streets where it's legal and low-impact — this is where we live.

That said, travel months look different. When we're moving between regions, we'll occasionally pay for a campsite — for a shower, for a hook-up, for a spot that just makes logistical sense. In those months, overnight costs can creep up to $100 or more. Budget accordingly if you're planning a big move.

Learning where to find free camping is one of the most valuable skills you'll build as a van lifer. Apps like iOverlander, The Dyrt, and Campendium have been part of our toolkit for years.

## Groceries: inflation is real and we're adapting

We're spending $350–$400/month on food for two people. That's not effortless — we're actively shopping sales, buying what's marked down, and not wasting anything.

Van cooking has its own rhythm. You learn to use what you have, meal plan loosely, and get creative with a two-burner stove. We eat well. We just do it intentionally.

## Insurance: van and health

Van insurance runs us $120/month. Health insurance for both of us is $150/month each, so $300 total. These are non-negotiables — we've never skipped either and we wouldn't recommend it.

Health insurance is the one that trips people up before they leave. If you're coming off an employer plan, you'll need to figure out your coverage before you go. The cost varies wildly depending on your state of domicile, age, and plan.

## Phone & internet: $137/month for both

Reliable internet isn't optional when you work from the road. We're at $137/month for both our phone plans combined, which includes mobile data we use as our primary connection.

This is one area where new van lifers sometimes overspend out of anxiety. You don't need every carrier. Start with one solid plan and adjust.

## What we don't spend money on (that most people do)

No rent. No utilities. No gym membership. No storage unit. No car payment — the van is paid off. These are the line items that quietly make van life financially viable for us in a way that traditional housing no longer would be.

We've also paid off debt since starting this life, and we're saving. That wasn't true in year one. It became true because we stayed, paid attention, and kept trimming what wasn't serving us.

## How our costs have changed over 8 years

Year one, we were figuring it out — over-buying gear, making inefficient decisions, not yet knowing where to find free camping or how to batch our errands. We spent more and got less.

Now, we spend less and get more. We know our van intimately. We know our habits. We've stripped out the non-essentials and kept what actually makes the life good. Financial confidence on the road is a skill, and like any skill, it takes time to develop.

## So, can you afford it?

Possibly more than you think. Possibly less than the Instagram version suggests. The real number — for us, right now — lands somewhere between $1,200 and $1,500 a month before unexpected expenses. Your number will be different depending on how much you drive, where you park, and what you've decided matters.

What we can tell you after eight years is this: the financial learning curve is real, and so is the payoff. The life gets leaner and richer at the same time, if you let it.
