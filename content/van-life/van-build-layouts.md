After eight years of living full-time in our van, we've made a lot of decisions we'd make the same way again — and at least one major one we'd do completely differently. Our van build layout is the latter.

When we were planning our build, we researched endlessly. We looked at every Instagram tour, every YouTube walkthrough, every blog post we could find. We thought we had it figured out. And in a lot of ways, our layout is actually really smart — it maximizes space beautifully. But smart on paper and livable every single day are two very different things, and that gap is what this post is about.

If you're in the planning stages of your van build, consider this your shortcut. We're sharing the layouts we think work best for full-time van dwelling, why we believe in them, and the honest lessons we learned from living in a layout that didn't quite fit our life.

A quick note: this post is written specifically for people building a van they plan to live in full-time. Full-time living means your van is your home — not a weekend escape, not a seasonal adventure vehicle. Because of that, comfort features matter more here than they might for part-time builds. These recommendations are based on our experience after nearly a decade on the road.

## Start here: the question that shapes everything

Before you decide on a single layout detail, ask yourself one thing: will I be working remotely from this van?

If the answer is yes — whether you're employed remotely or running your own business from the road — your workspace is not a nice-to-have. It's infrastructure. You need a place where you can sit comfortably for hours, focus, and feel like a functional human being, not someone hunched over a laptop on a couch cushion at a weird angle.

This is something we thought we'd nailed in our own build. We knew we'd be working from the van constantly, so we prioritized a convertible bed-couch-workspace setup that could do it all. In theory, it was brilliant. In practice, it became one of our biggest daily frustrations — and we'll get into exactly why in a minute.

## Our layout: the convertible setup (and why we're moving on)

Our current van has a U-shaped convertible layout. When it's set up as a couch, it seats us plus two or three other people comfortably — which is genuinely great for hosting friends on the road. When it's converted to a bed, it's spacious. And as a workspace, it gives us room to spread out.

On paper, it's one of the most space-efficient layouts you can do in a van. We still believe that.

The problem is what it requires of you every single day.

To go from sleeping to working, we have to convert. To go from working to sleeping, we have to convert again. That's making the bed — in the traditional sense — twice a day, except instead of pulling up a comforter, we're moving cushions, rearranging components, and reorganizing the space entirely. After a long day of hiking or driving or exploring, this task is the last thing either of us wants to do.

So what actually happens? We often just don't convert. We work from bed, which sounds charming and remote-work-dreamy until you've done it for three days straight and your back is screaming at you. Or we leave the van in couch mode and sleep on a surface that feels slightly different than a proper bed because it's made up of separate cushions rather than one continuous mattress.

A comfortable, consistent sleep surface turned out to matter to us more than we anticipated. It might matter to you too.

We share this not to scare you off the convertible layout — it genuinely works well for some people, especially if you have guests often or need to maximize seating. But if the idea of converting your living space twice a day already sounds annoying to you just reading about it, trust that instinct. Before you commit to a layout, try renting a van with a similar setup for a few days. One weekend of living in a space will tell you more than months of research.

## The case for a fixed bed layout

If we were building today — and we will be building again soon — we'd choose a fixed bed without question.

A fixed bed means your sleeping area stays a sleeping area. You pull back the covers in the morning and leave them there. You make the bed once, the way you would in a house, and it stays made. Your sheets live on the bed. Your pillows stay on the bed. It's just a bed. A real one.

This sounds so simple that it almost feels silly to list as a selling point. But when you're living in 80 square feet, the things that create daily friction matter enormously. Eliminating even one recurring task — especially one that stands between you and sleep — has a real impact on your quality of life on the road.

**Pros of a fixed bed**

- A genuinely comfortable sleep surface — one continuous mattress, not cushion segments
- No daily conversion required; your bed stays made and ready
- Clearly defined sleeping and sitting zones, which makes the van feel more like a home
- More under-bed storage — what van lifers call the "garage" — for electrical components, a water tank, gear, and bikes
- The potential for a small secondary sleeping area for guests, depending on layout
- Day-to-day functionality that makes full-time living significantly easier

**Cons of a fixed bed**

- Takes up more square footage than a convertible setup
- Your dedicated seating area may be smaller depending on the rest of the build
- The bed tends to sit higher (closer to the ceiling), which can be a consideration if you're tall

For most full-time van dwellers, the trade-offs are worth it. The fixed bed wins on livability — and livability is everything when this is your home, not just your weekend.

## Other layout considerations worth planning around

### Your workspace

Whether you're fully remote or just need a place to catch up on emails, you want a dedicated spot to sit and work that isn't your bed. A small table or dinette setup that doesn't require full conversion is ideal — swivel seats, a fold-down table attached to the wall, or a compact dinette that doubles as a meal area.

The goal is a seat, a surface, and enough light — without dismantling your entire living situation to access it.

### Your shower situation

Shower decisions are deeply personal in van life. A lot of people use gym memberships, campground facilities, or truck stops as their primary option — and that works great until you're three days into a dispersed camping stretch with no amenities nearby.

An indoor shower takes up meaningful real estate, so how much you build in (if at all) should reflect how often you realistically think you'll use it. Options range from a full wet bath with a proper shower to a compact outdoor shower mounted on the exterior, an indoor portable shower, or a cassette toilet with a separate rinse setup.

### Kitchen space

You will cook in this van more than you think you will. Even if you're someone who currently relies on restaurants, life on the road has a way of shifting that — partly because good restaurants aren't always nearby, and partly because cooking in a van is actually enjoyable once you have a decent setup.

Prioritize a sink with enough depth to actually wash dishes, counter space to prep a real meal, and storage for a reasonable amount of pantry staples. Cramped kitchen setups are one of the most common regrets we hear from van lifers who rushed this part of the build.

### Storage — all of it

Build in more storage than you think you need, then add a little more. Overhead cabinets, under-bed garage space, wall pockets, drawer systems — every cubic inch counts when you're living full-time. The layouts that work best over the long haul are the ones where everything has a home and you're not constantly shuffling gear to access what you need.

## What we'd include in an ideal full-time layout

Based on eight years of living this life, here's what we consider non-negotiable:

- A fixed bed with a quality, continuous mattress
- A dedicated seating area with a table for work and meals — no conversion required
- Under-bed garage storage (electrical components, water tank, gear)
- A kitchen with a proper sink and enough counter space to cook real food
- Some form of shower solution, even if it's simple
- Overhead and wall storage throughout
- Enough ceiling height to sit up in bed comfortably

We'd also strongly suggest building for your real daily routine, not your ideal one. If you know you hate making the bed, don't build a layout that requires you to do it twice. If you know you work long hours, invest in a proper workspace. The van that serves you best is the one designed around how you actually live — not how you imagine you might.

## A note on van size

All of the layouts we've described here are best suited to a longer wheelbase van — a 170" Sprinter, an extended Transit, or an extended ProMaster (which is what we drive). Shorter wheelbase vans can absolutely be built out well, but you'll have fewer options for separating zones and fitting everything comfortably.

If you're still deciding on a van platform before you even get to layout planning, that's a whole conversation worth having before you fall in love with a layout that won't physically fit your vehicle.

Whatever layout you land on — we hope it feels like home from day one.
