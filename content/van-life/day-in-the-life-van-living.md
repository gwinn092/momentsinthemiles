People imagine one of two things when they picture a day in a van. Either it's golden hour on a cliff somewhere, coffee in hand, laptop open to an inbox that only contains good news. Or it's chaos — cramped, dirty, one wrong turn from a breakdown.

The real version lives somewhere in between, and it shifts depending on the day. Some days we're parked somewhere so beautiful it still doesn't feel real. Some days we're in a Planet Fitness parking lot in a city we don't love because the wifi is reliable and we needed a shower.

Here's what a real day actually looks like — not the highlight, not the horror story. The composite.

## Morning: the first thing that happens is not romantic

Before coffee, before checking where we are or what the light looks like outside, there's a mental inventory. How's the battery? Did we leave the fan running? Is it hot enough in here that we slept badly, or did we actually sleep? How's the water situation?

This takes about thirty seconds and happens automatically now. Eight years in, it's just the first thing your brain does. Like checking your phone, except it's checking your home.

Then coffee. Always coffee. We have a setup we're genuinely proud of — the kind of morning coffee that made us stop apologizing for living in a van. Whoever's up first makes it. That's the only firm rule we have.

**Somewhere around 7 or 8am**

If we're parked somewhere worth being outside, we're outside. Coffee, whatever we're reading, maybe just sitting. This is the part that earns its place on Instagram because it genuinely is that good.

If we're in a city or a parking lot or somewhere that doesn't call for lingering, we're inside with the side door cracked and the fan running and we're already thinking about the day. Either version is fine. We stopped needing every morning to be scenic a long time ago.

---

## Work: yes, we actually work. A lot.

This is the part that surprises people most. Van life isn't a vacation. It's a location — one that moves. We run two websites, manage content, handle affiliate partnerships, answer emails, and do all the same stuff anyone working remotely does, except our office has a windshield and occasionally someone knocks on the window to ask if we're okay.

**Morning block — usually 8 or 9 to noon**

This is heads-down time. Jesse's usually on site builds or technical stuff. I'm writing, scheduling, working on content. We're in the same 80 square feet but largely in our own worlds. Headphones help. Having a coffee setup we love helps more.

Wifi is the variable that shapes everything. Good signal and the day flows. Spotty signal and you're making decisions — drive somewhere better, use a hotspot, accept that today is a lighter day. After enough years you get good at planning around it rather than fighting it.

---

## Midday: errands, movement, the unsexy stuff

Van life has a list of recurring logistics that don't go away: water, laundry, groceries, dumping tanks if you have a toilet, finding a shower if you need one, mail if anything's been forwarded. None of it is hard. All of it takes time.

**Town days — a few times a week**

We pick a home base — usually a library or a coffee shop with good wifi — and work from there while we knock out whatever needs knocking out. Laundry runs itself. Groceries get done. The van gets topped off with water if we're running low.

There's a rhythm to it that took maybe a year to find and now just feels like Tuesday. The logistics that seemed overwhelming before we started are mostly just Tuesday now.

---

## Afternoon: this is when it gets good

If the morning is work and the midday is logistics, the afternoon is usually ours. This is the part of van life that actually looks like the version people imagine.

**2 or 3pm onward**

A hike we didn't plan. A town we stopped in because something looked interesting from the highway. A long slow drive down a road that wasn't on any list. Sometimes just reading in a camp chair next to the van while the light changes.

We've been doing this long enough that we don't feel pressure to optimize the afternoon. Some of our best days have been the ones where we didn't do anything worth posting about. We just existed somewhere and it was enough.

---

## Evening: finding a spot, making food, settling in

If we're moving, late afternoon is when we start thinking about where we're sleeping. This used to feel stressful. Now it's just part of the day — check the apps, think about what we need (hookups? dispersed camping? just a quiet street?), and point toward it.

**5 or 6pm**

Dinner in the van is genuinely one of our favorite things. We've gotten good at cooking in a small space, and there's something about a meal you made yourself in a place you chose that hits differently than almost anything else. We eat outside when we can. Inside when we can't. Either way, it's ours.

Evenings are slow. We're not night people. We read, we watch something if we have signal, we talk. The days that started with a battery check and a parking lot coffee somehow always seem to end well.

---

## The part nobody shows you

There are days that don't have a good narrative arc. Days where the spot we planned fell through and we ended up somewhere fine but not special. Days where we both worked too long and didn't go anywhere. Days where something broke and the whole afternoon became about fixing it.

Those days exist. They're real. And honestly? Even those days — the flat ones, the frustrating ones, the ones that didn't produce a single photo worth keeping — we're still living them in a van, on our terms, in a life we built on purpose.

That's the part that's hard to explain until you've felt it. Even the ordinary days feel like yours.

That's the whole thing, really.
