There's a version of van life that lives on the internet. You've probably seen it.

Golden hour through the back doors. Coffee in hand, mountains behind you. A perfectly styled interior that somehow stays clean despite the fact that two people, all their belongings, and the general chaos of daily life exist inside 80 square feet of moving vehicle. The caption says something like "just another Tuesday" and it gets 14,000 likes.

We've been doing this for eight years. And we're here to tell you — that version is real. It just isn't the whole picture.

## What social media gets right (and wrong) about van life

Here's the thing about the highlight reel: it's not dishonest, exactly. Those golden hour moments happen. The stunning campsites are real. There are mornings that feel so good you genuinely can't believe this is your life.

But social media, by design, shows you the peak. The algorithm rewards beauty, aspiration, and ease. It doesn't reward the Tuesday you spent four hours in a Walmart parking lot waiting for a part to arrive. It doesn't reward the week it rained every single day and you couldn't open the windows and everything felt slightly damp and slightly tense. It doesn't reward the conversation you had to have about money, or space, or what you're actually doing with your life.

Those moments happen too. They happen a lot, actually. And somewhere along the way, a gap opened up between what van life looks like online and what van life actually is — and that gap has quietly convinced a lot of people that they're doing it wrong.

## The misconception that does the most damage

Of all the things social media gets wrong about van life, the most damaging isn't the aesthetic. It's the implication that it's easy.

When every van you see online is immaculate, every campsite is picturesque, and every couple looks like they're genuinely thriving — it creates an unconscious standard. And when your reality doesn't match that standard, it's easy to assume the problem is you.

We've talked to people who quit van life after a few months because they thought they were failing at it. They weren't. They were just living it.

Real van life includes:

**The logistical weight nobody posts about.** Finding water. Dumping tanks. Figuring out where you're sleeping tonight when the spot you planned fell through. Managing mail, insurance, and healthcare without a fixed address. These aren't glamorous problems, but they're consistent ones — and they take real time and real energy to solve.

**The relational friction that gets edited out.** Living in a small space with another person is an intimacy accelerant. It surfaces things. The version of van life you see online tends to feature two people who seem effortlessly aligned — same pace, same vision, same energy. What you don't see is the negotiation that makes that possible. The checking in. The giving each other space in a space that doesn't have much of it.

**The days that just feel hard.** Not dramatically hard. Just flat. Just ordinary. Just another day of doing the same things you'd do anywhere — laundry, work, groceries — except the logistics are more complicated and there's no couch big enough to disappear into.

None of this means van life isn't worth it. For us, after eight years, it absolutely still is. But it means something different than the version online suggests.

## What van life actually looks like

It looks like a life. A real one.

It looks like figuring things out in real time, often without a backup plan. It looks like building routines in an environment that keeps changing. It looks like getting good at solving small problems quickly, because small problems in a van have a way of becoming bigger ones if you ignore them.

It looks like knowing your partner's moods by the sound of their breathing. It looks like waking up somewhere beautiful and feeling genuinely grateful, and waking up somewhere not beautiful and feeling genuinely okay with that too.

It looks like the kind of life where the hard parts and the good parts aren't separate categories — they're the same life, running together, and you're in the middle of it.

That part doesn't photograph as well. But it's the part that actually changes you.

## Permission to let it be messy

If you've been watching van life content and quietly wondering why yours doesn't look like that — this is your sign that it's not supposed to.

The people with the beautiful content aren't living a different van life than you. They're just showing you a different slice of it. And the slice they're choosing to show is the one that performs.

What social media can't show you — and what eight years on the road has taught us — is that the version of van life worth having isn't the one that looks good in the frame. It's the one that feels like yours. Messy logistics and all.

You don't have to earn van life by making it look effortless. You're allowed to be in it, fully, even on the days that don't make the feed.

That's the part nobody talks about enough. And honestly? It's the best part.
