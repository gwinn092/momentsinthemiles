This Is Us

Hey there — we're Jesse and Karlee.

Long before this lifestyle had a name, we were chasing dirt roads. Weekends meant car camping through aspen groves, hiking red rock canyons, and driving into stretches of desert that felt endless. Somewhere between long highway conversations and quiet sunsets, we realized those miles weren't just escapes — they were shaping us.

They felt natural. Steady. Like the life we wanted wasn't somewhere else — it was already unfolding in motion.

As rent climbed and the noise of ordinary life grew louder, we started asking better questions. Not dramatic ones. Just honest ones.

Why are we waiting for permission to live the way we already know feels right?

So we bought a van.

We traded square footage for open roads. Certainty for flexibility. A fixed address for something that could move as freely as we did.

This wasn't escapism. It was alignment.

What began as a practical decision became a different way of living — one that values experience over accumulation, curiosity over autopilot, and movement over standing still.

And then it expanded.

Four wheels led to flight paths. Road trips stretched beyond state lines. Home stopped being a location and became something we carried with us.

Before we built this life together, we each wandered independently. We both backpacked through Europe. Jesse spent time in South Africa. Karlee lived for five years on the island of St. John. Travel shaped us individually long before it shaped us as a couple.

Together, it deepened.

We've crisscrossed the continental United States, spent seven years living inside a van, and moved slowly through Southeast Asia for eight months — sleeping under more stars than streetlights. We've taken wrong turns, miscalculated budgets, adjusted plans, and learned to move with intention instead of impulse.

Travel has expanded our world. Partnership has steadied it.

Moments in the Miles is where it all connects.

This is a reflective travel and lifestyle blog — part lived journal, part practical guide. It's where we share the full picture: messy mornings in a cramped galley kitchen, backroads that led to better stories, the financial realities that make this sustainable, and the quiet conviction required to choose mobility over convention.

We don't romanticize the road. We don't pretend it's always easy.

But we do believe this:

When you slow down enough, your life starts to feel intentional again.

Travel, done thoughtfully, makes you more aware — of privilege, of fragility, of how much comfort we assume and how little we actually need.

Van life was the spark. Travel became the fire.

Our goal isn't to convince you to change your address. It's to show how one brave decision can ripple outward into a different way of seeing your life.

There is more than one way to build something meaningful.

You're not behind. You're not late. You're allowed to choose differently.

If you're questioning what success looks like — if you're craving depth over speed — if you want inspiration grounded in real experience — you're in the right place.

Welcome to Moments in the Miles.

This Is Karlee

My love of adventure didn't start with me. It was passed down.

I grew up in a small town in upstate New York — a single mother, two houses down from my grandparents. As an only child, they were my constants. My grandmother was my second mother in every way that mattered. Short hair, glasses, always somewhere between the Persian cats she raised and the crafting room that took up half the house. Fierce and loving and completely unapologetic about both. My grandfather was quieter — scruffy, always working on a car or a motorcycle, not a man who needed many words. But his heart made up for every one he didn't say. I remember sitting on the kitchen table with him as a kid, the two of us eating ice cream straight out of the carton. No bowls. No occasion. Just us.

If they were going somewhere, I was going too. Cross-country road trips. Weekend camping adventures. Long drives with no real destination. They showed me early that the world was bigger than our small town — and that movement was something to be sought, not feared.

They also had a plan. Work hard. Save carefully. Buy the camper. Travel the country. See the family in Colorado without it being a whole production. Finally have the time to truly live.

Retirement was when life would begin.

My grandmother got sick before that chapter ever opened. They told her it was asthma at first. By the time anyone looked closer, the cancer had spread into her bones. I was sixteen. I went to school, I went to work, and I went to her — because that was all I knew how to do. Just show up and be near her, because being near her had always been enough before.

She passed away before the camper ever happened. And after that, my grandfather was never quite the same.

I watched decades of careful planning fail to guarantee more time. I watched the window close quietly, without ceremony. And something in me shifted permanently.

There is no guarantee of later.

That truth didn't break me. It clarified me. It became the lens through which I've made almost every important decision since.

In my mid-twenties, after a six-year relationship ended, I found myself stuck. Heartbroken in a small town, winter closing in, unsure of who I was without the life I had planned. The kind of lost that doesn't announce itself — it just settles in and gets comfortable.

Then my friend Keara called from St. John.

She had a job for me. Come stay. Just come.

I hesitated. It felt reckless. Dramatic. The kind of thing other people did.

Then I thought about the camper. About the plan that was always going to start later. About my grandmother in that house two doors down, always mid-project, always present, gone before she got to go.

I stopped waiting.

I packed a carry-on, grabbed my pit bull Loki, and two days later I was on a plane.

I remember flying in low over the ocean toward St. Thomas — watching the water below me shift from deep navy to something almost unreal, a blue that doesn't have a name in ordinary life. I had no idea exactly where I was going. I collected Loki from cargo, let him stretch his legs, and we crammed into the back of a truck that took us to the far end of the island for the ferry to St. John. The roads were narrow and hilly and completely indifferent to anyone's nerves. The humidity was heavy — the kind that wraps around you before you've even decided how you feel about it.

I took my welcome shot of rum at the ferry dock.

Keara picked us up on the other side. We dropped my things at the apartment — a blow up mattress in the corner of the living room, a view of the ocean from the window — and two hours later I was on shift at my first job, trying to figure out how to communicate with a kitchen that didn't speak my language.

I got home that night and curled up with Loki on that mattress.

Everything was different. Everything was strange. But I had him. And outside the window, the ocean was still there, exactly where I'd left it.

I didn't regret a single bit of it.

St. John is eight square miles. Seventy percent of it is national park. The locals call it Love City — and after five years there, I understand completely why.

I arrived a broken girl. I left a woman who knew her worth.

The island didn't fix me. It gave me the space to stop performing. There was no one to impress, no role to fill, no version of myself I was expected to maintain. I did what I wanted, when I wanted, how I wanted. I stopped asking for permission I never needed to ask for in the first place. I stopped shrinking.

I learned something there that I've carried everywhere since:

Sometimes the best way to figure out who you are is to get to the place where you don't have to be anything else.

The person I had been was still in there — she had just been caged up. St. John opened the door.

I met some of the most real and loving people of my life on that island. The kind of friendships that don't require explanation or history — just presence. Some of my closest friends in the world came from those five years. The island gives that to you, if you let it.

I left with a sense of calm I hadn't known before. A certainty about what I was capable of. A refusal to settle for less than a life that actually felt alive.

That decision didn't make me fearless. It made me intentional.

Before St. John, I had already wandered. Europe in my early twenties — backpacking, figuring things out, collecting the kind of experiences that quietly shape you before you know they're doing it. Travel had already found me. But St. John was where I found myself.

Every adventure that followed traces back to that ferry dock. The van. Forty-eight states. Eight months across Southeast Asia. All of it grew from the same root — a woman who stopped waiting for permission and started building a life that felt alive.

I don't chase escape. I chase expansion.

I travel because I've seen what happens when you wait. And I refuse to wait.

If something in your life has been sitting in a cage — a dream, a decision, a version of yourself you haven't let out yet — you're in the right place.

The door is already open. You just have to walk through it.

XO, Karlee
